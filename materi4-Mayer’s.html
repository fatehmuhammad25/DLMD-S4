Picture Description and Mayer's Social Constructivism

In teaching and learning, teachers must help students understand new lessons easily. One useful theory is Multimedia Learning Theory by Richard E. Mayer. This theory explains how students learn better by using pictures, words, and sounds together.

In this explanation, I will describe how my teaching method called Picture Description follows the ideas in Mayer’s Multimedia Learning Theory, especially the parts about Dual-channel processing and how to use it in E-Learning.

Multimedia Learning Theory

Multimedia Learning Theory is a theory by Richard E. Mayer. He believed that students can learn better if teachers use both pictures and words when teaching. If we only use words or only pictures, students may find it difficult to understand. But if we combine both, students can learn more easily and remember longer.

What is Dual-Channel Processing?

Dual-channel processing means people have two ways to learn new information in their brain:

  • One for visual things, like pictures and videos.
  • One for auditory things, like sounds and spoken words.

When a teacher uses both pictures and sounds together, students can learn better because both channels in their brain are working at the same time. But the teacher must also be careful not to give too much information at once, or students can get confused.

General Application in E-Learning

This means using this theory when teaching online or using digital media. In e-learning, teachers can use pictures, videos, audio, PowerPoint slides, and other multimedia tools to help students learn. Teachers must make sure these tools are clear and simple, so students can easily understand the lesson without feeling too heavy in their minds.

How Picture Description Matches Multimedia Learning Theory

I use Picture Description in my teaching, especially for English speaking and writing. In this activity, students look at a picture and describe what they see. This activity follows Multimedia Learning Theory in these ways:

1. Dual-Channel Processing in Picture Description

In Picture Description, students:

  • Look at a picture → this uses the visual channel.
  • Listen to instructions and explanations from the teacher → this uses the auditory channel.
  • Speak or write their description → this helps connect both channels.

For example:

  • I show a picture of a park on the screen.
  • I say, “What can you see in the picture?”
  • I give an example: “I can see a tree.”
  • Then students look and speak or write about other things they see.

By using both pictures (visual) and spoken instructions (auditory), students learn better. It makes the learning activity interesting and easier to remember.

2. General Application in E-Learning

When teaching online or using digital media, I can use Picture Description in these ways:

  • Show digital pictures or slides in Zoom or Google Meet.
  • Give voice instructions through a video or online class.
  • Use simple animations or pictures with clear audio in a video.
  • Ask students to describe pictures through an online chat or video call.

By doing this:

  • Students use their visual and auditory channels.
  • The teacher controls the amount of information so it’s not too much.
  • Students stay focused and can enjoy the lesson.

For example, in an online class:

  • I show a simple picture through screen share.
  • I say, “What can you see here?”
  • I give them time to think.
  • Then I ask them one by one to describe it.
  • I give feedback directly through the mic or chat.

This makes the lesson easy to follow and helps students feel comfortable even in e-learning situations.

Example of the Activity

  1. Show a picture of a zoo on Zoom.
  2. Say: “What animals can you see in this picture?”
  3. Give an example: “I can see a lion.”
  4. Let students answer one by one through mic or chat.
  5. Give short feedback.

In this activity:

  • Picture = visual channel
  • Teacher’s voice and student answers = auditory channel

This matches Mayer’s Dual-channel processing principle.

Conclusion

In conclusion, my teaching method, Picture Description, follows Mayer’s Multimedia Learning Theory. It uses Dual-channel processing by combining pictures and spoken words in the activity. It also works well in E-Learning because I can use digital pictures, videos, and audio to help students learn online.

By using this method, students can understand better, feel more interested, and remember the lesson longer. I believe this is a simple and effective way to teach English speaking and writing, especially for beginners.

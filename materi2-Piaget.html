Picture Description and Piaget's Social Constructivism

In the teaching and learning process, it is important to help students become active learners. They should not only listen to the teacher but also explore and find information by themselves. One of the learning theories that supports this idea is Cognitive Constructivism by Jean Piaget. This theory says that students learn best when they build their own knowledge through experience and active participation.

What is Cognitive Constructivism?

Cognitive Constructivism is a learning theory by Jean Piaget. He believed that students are active when they learn. They are not like empty glasses that only get information from the teacher. But, they learn through experience, thinking, and trying things by themselves.

Piaget also said that students’ thinking grows in four stages:

  • Sensorimotor Stage (0–2 years old)
  • Preoperational Stage (2–7 years old)
  • Concrete Operational Stage (7–11 years old)
  • Formal Operational Stage (11 years old and above)

Each stage has a different way of thinking. So, teachers need to give activities that match students’ thinking stage.

What is Picture Description?

Picture Description is a method where students look at a picture and describe what they see. They can say or write about the objects, people, actions, or situations in the picture. This method is simple but very useful to improve students’ vocabulary, speaking, and writing skills.

When students describe a picture, they need to think about the picture, remember what they already know, and create new sentences. This is an active learning process, which is very suitable for Piaget’s theory.

How Picture Description Matches Cognitive Constructivism:

  1. Active Learning
    Cognitive Constructivism says that students must be active in learning. They should not only listen but also do something in the learning process. In Picture Description, students look at the picture, observe it carefully, and describe it using their own words. This activity makes students become active, not passive.
  2. Assimilation and Accommodation
    According to Piaget, students learn through two processes:
    • Assimilation: when students use their old knowledge to understand new things.
    • Accommodation: when students need to change their old knowledge because of new information.
    In Picture Description, students see a picture and use their old knowledge (assimilation) to explain it. If they find something new or strange, they adjust their understanding (accommodation) by learning new words or asking questions.
  3. Learning by Experience
    Piaget believed that students learn better through direct experience. They need to touch, see, hear, and feel things around them. Picture Description gives students a chance to learn by experience. They observe a picture, think about it, and explain it in their own way.
  4. Match with Students’ Thinking Stage
    Piaget said students have different ways of thinking in each stage. Most students in junior and senior high school are in the Concrete Operational Stage (7–11 years old) and Formal Operational Stage (11 years and above).
    • In the Concrete Operational Stage, students can describe pictures of real things, like animals, places, or daily activities.
    • In the Formal Operational Stage, students can describe abstract or complicated pictures, such as social situations or environmental problems.
  5. Learning by Doing (Discovery Learning)
    Piaget believed in Discovery Learning. It means students must find out things by themselves. In Picture Description, students look at a picture, think about what it shows, and explain it. They learn new words and ideas by themselves through this activity.

In conclusion, my teaching and learning approach uses Picture Description because it matches the principles of Cognitive Constructivism by Jean Piaget. This method makes students active, lets them learn through experience, helps them connect old and new knowledge, and is suitable for their thinking stage. Through this activity, students can improve their speaking and writing skills while also developing their thinking ability. By using this method, I believe students will enjoy the learning process more and become independent learners. They will not only remember what the teacher says but also build their own knowledge in their own way.
